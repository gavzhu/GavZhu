Dear Data: Sleep, Strain,
and Caloric Analysis

A five-day exploration of physiological data through three handcrafted visualizations, investigating whether "more" always means "better" in fitness metrics.

By Gavin Zhu • February 13, 2026

Central Theme: Quality vs Quantity in Fitness Metrics

This project explores a central question that emerged from my experience tracking physiological data with a WHOOP wearable: Does "more" always mean "better" when it comes to fitness metrics?

Conventional wisdom suggests that more sleep leads to better recovery, more exercise burns more calories, and longer workouts build more fitness. But as someone who tracks granular data daily, I began noticing patterns that challenged these assumptions.

Each question examines a different domain—recovery, energy, and effort—but all investigate the relationship between quantity and quality. By collecting data across the same five-day period and analyzing these questions in sequence, I aimed to uncover whether the "quality vs. quantity" tension appears consistently across my body's performance metrics.

  1. Q1 Sleep: Does sleeping longer result in proportionally more restorative sleep?
  2. Q2 Energy: How does daily calorie expenditure vary between rest and training days?
  3. Q3 Training Load: Which workout types contribute most to weekly strain?
Visualization 01

Sleep Stage Composition and Restorative Efficiency

Does sleeping longer result in proportionally more restorative (deep + REM) sleep?

Data Collection

I collected five consecutive nights of sleep data (Sunday, February 8 through Thursday, February 12, 2026) using a WHOOP 4.0 wearable device, which I wear continuously on my wrist. The WHOOP strap uses an optical heart rate sensor sampling at 100Hz and a 3-axis accelerometer to passively track sleep without requiring any manual input.

For my analysis, I combined deep sleep and REM sleep into a single "restorative sleep" category, as both stages are associated with physical recovery, memory consolidation, and cognitive restoration.

Visualization
Stacked bar chart created in Notability showing sleep stage composition. Purple represents restorative sleep (deep + REM), light blue represents light sleep.
Process & Design

I created a stacked bar chart visualization using the Notability app on iPad with Apple Pencil, combining the tactile quality of hand-drawing with the precision of digital tools. Each bar represents one night of sleep, with total bar height proportional to total sleep duration. The darker purple shade for restorative sleep and lighter blue for light sleep creates intuitive visual mapping—deeper, more restorative sleep appears visually "deeper" in tone.

Drawing Sunday's 10.5-hour bar and seeing how much of it was pale blue (light sleep) compared to Monday's compact 6-hour bar with its larger proportion of dark purple (restorative sleep) made the counterintuitive finding tangible in a way that an auto-generated chart would not.

Key Finding

Counterintuitive Result

Sleeping longer does not result in proportionally more restorative sleep. There is a moderate negative correlation (r = -0.55) between total sleep duration and restorative sleep percentage.

Physical visualization using uncooked white rice. Scale: 1 tablespoon = 100 calories. Each pile is labeled with day, calorie count, and tablespoon measurement.
Process & Design

I created a physical visualization using uncooked white rice, with each day's calorie expenditure represented by a proportional pile. I used a scale of 1 tablespoon of rice = 100 calories, making the measurements practical with standard kitchen tools while maintaining accurate proportions between days.

I chose rice as the physical medium because it creates an intuitive, tangible connection to energy—rice is itself a source of calories, making it a fitting material to represent energy expenditure. Physically measuring and pouring 17 tablespoons for Sunday versus 26 tablespoons for Wednesday made the difference visceral in a way that numbers on a screen cannot.

Visualization 03

Weekly Training Load by Activity Type

Which workout types contribute most to my weekly training load?

Data Collection

I collected five days of workout data using the WHOOP 4.0 wearable. WHOOP calculates a "strain" score for each workout on a 0-21 scale, derived from cardiovascular load based on heart rate data—specifically, time spent in different heart rate zones and overall cardiac effort during the activity.

Visualization
AI-generated visualization (Microsoft Bing Image Creator / DALL-E 3) with hand-added labels in Notability. Wave thickness represents proportional contribution to total strain.
Process & Design

I created this visualization using a hybrid approach: AI-generated artwork with manually added data labels. I used Microsoft Bing Image Creator (powered by DALL-E 3) to generate an abstract visualization, then added scientific labels in Notability.

Each activity is represented by a flowing wave, with wave thickness proportional to that activity's contribution to total weekly strain. The flowing, dynamic wave forms evoke movement, heart rate patterns, and the continuous nature of cardiovascular strain during exercise.

Key Finding

Volume vs Efficiency

Weightlifting dominates my weekly training load (45%) due to high volume (161 min), but Running is most efficient at 0.28 strain/minute—meaning a 58-minute run delivered nearly as much training stimulus as an 84-minute lifting session.

Synthesis

Comparing Media, Process, and Insights

Creating three visualizations using different media—digital hand-drawing in Notability, physical rice piles, and AI-generated artwork—revealed how profoundly the crafting process shapes one's relationship with data.

Sleep Visualization

Digital Hand-Drawing (Notability)

The digital sketching process required me to manually calculate and draw each stacked bar. This forced intimacy with the numbers made the counterintuitive finding visceral: I physically drew Sunday's bar nearly twice as tall, yet had to fill most of it with light blue.

Calorie Visualization

Physical Medium (Rice Piles)

The physical medium created the most memorable crafting experience. Measuring 17 tablespoons for Sunday versus 26 for Wednesday made the calorie difference tactile—I could feel the weight difference between rest and training days.

Strain Visualization

AI-Generated + Manual Labels

This hybrid approach offered the most striking visual result. The AI created flowing waves that evoke energy and movement far better than I could draw freehand. The tradeoff was less intimacy with the data during creation.

Effectiveness and Engagement

Most analytically clear: Visualization 1 (Sleep). The stacked bar format makes proportional comparisons immediate and precise.

Most viscerally engaging: Visualization 2 (Rice). There's something compelling about data you can touch.

Most aesthetically striking: Visualization 3 (Strain). The flowing waves create an emotional response—they look like energy, like heartbeats, like effort.

Common Thread: How the Questions Build on One Another

Extra Credit

The three questions were designed to sequentially probe the theme of quality vs. quantity in fitness metrics, with each finding informing the next:

Q1

Sleep established the foundational insight

Sleeping longer does not result in proportionally more restorative sleep. This revealed that efficiency and composition matter more than raw duration—a finding that challenged my assumption that "more is better."

↓
Q2

Calories tested whether this principle extends to energy

Training days burn significantly more calories than rest days, but the majority of daily burn comes from baseline metabolism regardless of activity. The foundation remains stable, while the "extra" is more variable.

↓
Q3

Strain deepened the analysis within training itself

While Weightlifting dominated total weekly strain (45%) due to volume, Running was most efficient at 0.28 strain/minute. Just as 6 hours of efficient sleep ≈ 10.5 hours, 58 minutes of running ≈ 84 minutes of lifting.

The Unified Insight

Whether examining sleep, energy, or training load, my data consistently reveals that quality and efficiency matter as much as—or more than—raw quantity. More hours of sleep, more calories burned, and more minutes of exercise do not automatically translate to proportionally better outcomes.

This finding has practical implications: rather than maximizing volume, I should optimize for efficiency and composition—prioritizing restorative sleep stages, strategic training days, and high-efficiency workouts when time is limited.
